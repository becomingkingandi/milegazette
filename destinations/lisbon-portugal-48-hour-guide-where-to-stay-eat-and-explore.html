Lisbon in 48 Hours: Where to Stay, Eat, and Explore Without the Crowds

Skip the tourist traps and see the real Lisbon with this no-nonsense two-day plan.

5 min read Destinations

Why Lisbon?

Lisbon is Europe's sunniest capital, with over 290 sunny days a year. It's also one of the cheapest Western European cities. A sit-down dinner with wine costs €15-25 per person. Public transit is €1.50 per ride. The city is walkable, hilly, and full of tile-covered buildings that glow in the afternoon light. I've visited three times in the last two years, and each trip gets better.

Where to Stay: Neighborhoods That Work

Skip the overpriced Baixa tourist zone. Stay in Alfama for authentic fado music and narrow streets (average Airbnb €80/night). Or try Príncipe Real for quieter, tree-lined squares and boutique hotels like the 10-room Casa d'Alfama (€120/night with breakfast). For nightlife, Bairro Alto has cheap apartments but loud bars until 3 AM. Book at least two weeks ahead for summer; prices jump 40% in July.

Day 1: Hills, Tiles, and Pasteis de Nata

Start at 8 AM at Manteigaria in Chiado for a fresh pastel de nata (€1.20). Walk to the Santa Justa Lift (€5.30 round trip, or skip the line by walking the uphill street behind it). Then head to the Miradouro da Graça viewpoint—free, less crowded than São Pedro de Alcântara. Lunch at Time Out Market (€12-18 for a meal; avoid weekends when it's packed). Afternoon: explore the Alfama alleys and catch fado at Tasca do Chico (€5 cover, €8 minimum drink).

Day 2: Belém and a Day Trip Option

Take tram 15E to Belém (€1.50, 20 minutes). See the Jerónimos Monastery (€10, book online to skip the 30-minute queue). Try the original pastel de nata at Pastéis de Belém (€1.30 each; buy a half-dozen to go). If you have extra time, take a 40-minute train to Sintra (€2.40) for the Pena Palace (€14, book 24 hours ahead). Return by 6 PM for sunset at the Miradouro da Senhora do Monte—best view of the city, free, and rarely crowded.

Where to Eat Without the Hype

For bacalhau (salt cod), go to Zé da Mouraria in Mouraria (€12 for a full plate). For seafood, Cervejaria Ramiro is famous but often has a 1-hour wait—go at 11:30 AM on a weekday. The grilled prawns (€18) are worth it. For a budget lunch, grab a bifana (pork sandwich) at O Trevo in Rossio (€3.50). Avoid restaurants with English menus on main squares; they charge double for half the quality.

Booking Tips to Save Money

Flights to Lisbon from the US start at $450 round-trip in January (use Google Flights price alerts). Book hotels on booking.com and filter by 'free cancellation'—prices drop 15% if you rebook 7 days before arrival. For trains, use cp.pt and book Intercidades tickets 5 days ahead for 30% discounts. Uber from the airport to central Lisbon is €8-12; the metro is €1.50 but requires a Viva Viagem card (€0.50).

Final Word: Go Now Before Prices Spike

Lisbon is still affordable, but that's changing fast. Hotel prices rose 22% in 2024, and tourist numbers hit record highs. Visit in March or October for mild weather (18-22°C) and 30% fewer crowds. Bring comfortable shoes—you'll walk 8-10 km per day. And don't forget: the best views are free, the best food is in side streets, and the best time to explore is early morning before the cruise ships dock.
