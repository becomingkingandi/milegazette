Budapest in 48 Hours: A No-Nonsense Itinerary for First-Timers

Two days in Budapest is tight, but with this itinerary you'll hit the highlights without rushing—and save money doing it.

5 min read Destinations

Why Budapest Works for a Short Trip

Budapest is compact, walkable, and cheap compared to other European capitals. The metro, tram, and bus system covers everything you need. A 72-hour travel card costs about $16 (5,500 HUF) and gets you unlimited rides. I've used it to hop between Buda and Pest in under 15 minutes. The city's layout—hilly Buda on one side, flat Pest on the other—makes sightseeing efficient. You can cover the core in two days if you plan smart.

Day 1: Buda Castle, Fisherman's Bastion, and a Thermal Bath

Start at Buda Castle. Take bus 16 from Deák Ferenc tér to the castle hill—it's free with your travel card. Walk the grounds by 9 a.m. before crowds hit. Then head to Fisherman's Bastion (free entry to the lower terraces; upper towers cost $3). The view over Parliament is worth the short climb. By noon, take the tram down to Gellért Baths. Entry is $20 (7,000 HUF) on weekdays. Soak in the outdoor pools for two hours. It's less touristy than Széchenyi and just as good. Grab lunch at a nearby bistro—try the goulash at Gettó Gulyás for $12.

Day 2: Pest Side, Parliament, and Ruin Bars

Day two is all about Pest. Start at the Hungarian Parliament Building. Book a guided tour online ($10) to see the crown jewels. The interior is over-the-top gold and marble. Walk over to St. Stephen's Basilica (free entry, $5 for the dome view). By lunch, head to the Great Market Hall. Grab a lángos (fried dough with sour cream) for $4. In the afternoon, explore the Jewish Quarter. It's packed with ruin bars like Szimpla Kert. Go around 5 p.m. for a quieter vibe. A pint of local beer costs $3. Save the party for later if you want.

Where to Eat Without Blowing Your Budget

Street food is your friend. Langos at the market, chimney cake (kürtőskalács) from stalls for $3, and goulash at a local étkezde (canteen) for $8. For a sit-down meal, try Borkonyha Wine Kitchen. Their lunch menu is $15 for three courses. Avoid tourist traps on Váci utca. Walk two blocks over to Kazinczy Street for better prices and authentic food. I spent $25 total on food per day and ate well.

Getting Around and Saving Money

Use public transport. A single ticket is $1.20, but a block of 10 tickets costs $10. The Budapest Card ($30 for 48 hours) includes unlimited transit and free entry to some museums. I skipped it because I only visited two paid attractions. Walk where you can—the Chain Bridge is a 10-minute stroll between Buda and Pest. Uber isn't available; use Bolt for rides, but expect $5–8 for short trips. Taxis are fine if you use official ones (yellow plates).

Final Tips for a Smooth Visit

Bring cash. Many small shops and market stalls don't take cards. ATMs are everywhere but avoid Euronet ones—they charge high fees. Buy thermal bath tickets online to skip queues. Pack comfortable shoes; you'll walk 8–10 miles a day. And learn two words: köszönöm (thank you) and szia (hello). Locals appreciate it. For a 48-hour trip, this plan covers the essentials without burnout. You'll leave wanting more—and that's the point.
