Budapest in 48 Hours: A No-Nonsense Itinerary for First-Timers

Two days in Budapest is tight, but this itinerary packs in the Danube views, thermal baths, and ruin bars without the tourist traps.

5 min read Destinations

Why Budapest Works for a Short Trip

Budapest is compact and walkable, with efficient public transit. You can see the core sights in 48 hours without rushing. The city splits into Buda (hills, history) and Pest (nightlife, food). Budget tip: a 72-hour public transport pass costs about $15 and covers buses, trams, and the metro. I used it to hop between districts without wasting time.

Day 1: Buda Castle and Fisherman's Bastion

Start at Buda Castle. Take the funicular up ($5 one-way) or walk for free. The castle grounds are open 24/7, but the Hungarian National Gallery inside costs $10 and is worth it for the art. Next, head to Fisherman's Bastion. It's free to walk the terraces, and the views over Parliament are the best in the city. Go early—by 9 AM, crowds swell. Grab a chimney cake from a street vendor for $3.

Day 1 Afternoon: Széchenyi Thermal Bath

After lunch, take tram 4 or 6 to Széchenyi Thermal Bath. Entry is $20 for a full day. Bring your own towel to save $5 on rental. The outdoor pools are the highlight—even in winter, the steam rises off the 100°F water. I spent three hours here soaking and people-watching. Pro tip: rent a locker for $2 and leave your valuables inside.

Day 1 Evening: Ruin Bars in the Jewish Quarter

The Jewish Quarter is the epicenter of Budapest's nightlife. Szimpla Kert is the original ruin bar—a maze of mismatched furniture and graffiti. Drinks cost $4–$6. It's cash-only, so hit an ATM first. For a quieter vibe, try Instant, which has multiple rooms and a courtyard. Both are packed by 10 PM, so arrive at 8 PM to get a table.

Day 2: Parliament and the Great Market Hall

Start day two at the Hungarian Parliament Building. Book a tour online ($12) at least a week ahead—they sell out. The interior is gold-leafed and stunning. Afterward, walk 10 minutes to the Great Market Hall. Grab a langos (fried dough with sour cream and cheese) for $4. The upper floor has souvenirs and paprika—stock up here for $2 per bag.

Day 2 Afternoon: Danube Promenade and Shoes Memorial

Walk the Danube Promenade from Parliament to the Shoes on the Danube Bank memorial. It's free and moving—60 pairs of iron shoes commemorate Jews killed in 1944–45. Continue to the Chain Bridge, which connects Buda and Pest. Cross it for a photo op. If you have time, take a 1-hour river cruise ($10) for panoramic views.

Practical Tips for Your Budapest Trip

Budapest is affordable. A meal at a mid-range restaurant costs $10–$15. Use Revolut or Wise for fee-free card payments. Tipping is expected—10% is standard. Avoid taxis; use Bolt or public transit. The best time to visit is April–May or September–October for mild weather and fewer crowds. I went in late April and had sunny 65°F days.
