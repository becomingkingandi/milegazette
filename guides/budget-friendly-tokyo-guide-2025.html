Tokyo on $80 a Day: How to Explore Japan’s Capital Without Breaking the Bank

Discover how to navigate Tokyo’s neon-lit streets, savor world-class ramen, and visit iconic temples—all for $80 a day or less.

5 min read Guides

Why Tokyo Doesn’t Have to Be Expensive

Tokyo has a reputation for being pricey, but with smart planning, you can experience its best without draining your wallet. I spent a week here in 2024 on a $560 budget, including accommodation. The key is prioritizing what matters: food, culture, and transportation. Skip the luxury hotels and pricey sushi joints; instead, embrace convenience stores, public parks, and local trains. You’ll leave with memories, not debt.

Getting Around: The Suica Card Is Your Best Friend

Public transit in Tokyo is efficient and affordable. Get a Suica card at any JR station for 500 yen ($3.30) deposit. Load it with 3,000 yen ($20) for three days of unlimited travel on trains and buses. Avoid taxis—they start at 420 yen ($2.80) and add up fast. For longer trips, the Tokyo Metro 24-hour pass costs 600 yen ($4) and covers 13 lines. I used it to visit Shibuya, Asakusa, and Ueno in one day.

Where to Stay: Capsules and Hostels Under $40

For budget sleepers, capsule hotels and hostels are your go-to. I booked a capsule at Nine Hours Shinjuku for 3,500 yen ($23) per night—clean, quiet, and central. Hostels like Khaosan Tokyo Origami in Asakusa start at 2,500 yen ($17) for a dorm bed. Book on Booking.com or Hostelworld at least two weeks ahead for best rates. Avoid peak cherry blossom season (March–April) when prices double.

Eat Like a Local: Ramen, Onigiri, and Conbini Meals

Dining cheaply is easy. Grab onigiri (rice balls) at 7-Eleven for 120 yen ($0.80) each—try the tuna mayo flavor. For a hearty meal, visit Ichiran Ramen in Shibuya; a basic bowl costs 980 yen ($6.50). Street stalls in Tsukiji Market offer grilled skewers for 200–400 yen ($1.30–$2.70). I averaged 1,200 yen ($8) per day on food by sticking to conbini (convenience store) breakfasts and lunch specials.

Free and Cheap Attractions You Can’t Miss

Tokyo is packed with low-cost experiences. Meiji Shrine in Harajuku is free to enter—just a 10-minute walk from the station. The Imperial Palace East Gardens cost nothing and offer a peaceful escape. For skyline views, head to the Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building in Shinjuku; its observatory is free and open until 10 PM. I spent an afternoon there watching sunset over Mount Fuji.

Sample Budget Breakdown for One Day

Here’s what I spent on a typical day: Hostel capsule: 3,500 yen ($23). Suica card top-up: 600 yen ($4). Breakfast (onigiri and coffee from 7-Eleven): 300 yen ($2). Lunch (ramen at a local shop): 800 yen ($5.30). Dinner (bento box from FamilyMart): 500 yen ($3.30). Total: 5,700 yen ($38). With free attractions, I stayed under $80 easily. Add a museum ticket (like the Edo-Tokyo Museum at 600 yen) for more depth.

Final Tip: Cash Is Still King

Despite Tokyo’s modernity, many small shops and restaurants only accept cash. I withdrew 20,000 yen ($133) from an ATM at 7-Eleven (no fee with a Schwab card) and used it over five days. Credit cards work at major chains and hotels, but always carry yen for street food and temple donations. Plan your withdrawals to avoid foreign transaction fees—use a no-fee card like the Capital One VentureOne.
