Budapest on a Budget: How to Experience the City for Under $60 a Day

Discover how to enjoy Budapest's thermal baths, ruin bars, and Danube views for less than $60 a day.

5 min read Guides

Why Budapest Is a Budget Traveler’s Dream

Budapest offers European charm at a fraction of the cost of Paris or Rome. A hearty meal costs $8–$12, a pint of local beer runs $2, and public transport is under $1 per ride. With careful planning, you can cover accommodation, food, and activities for $60 a day—or less.

Find a Bed for $25 a Night

Skip the hotels and book a hostel in District VII (the Jewish Quarter). Maverick City Lodge offers clean dorms from $22 per night, while the stylish Carpe Noctem has beds starting at $25. For more privacy, Airbnb apartments near the city center average $35–$45 nightly. Book at least two weeks ahead for the best rates.

Eat Like a Local for $15 a Day

Start with a $3 breakfast at a local bakery (try lángos, a fried dough with sour cream and cheese). For lunch, grab a bowl of goulash at street-food stalls near the Great Market Hall for $6. Dinner at a traditional restaurant like Gettó Gulyás costs $8–$10. Avoid tourist strips near Váci Street—prices double there.

Free and Cheap Sights

Walk across the Széchenyi Chain Bridge for free views of the Danube. Explore Buda Castle’s courtyards without paying (the castle museum costs $12, but skip it). Visit the Hungarian Parliament building from outside—it’s free and stunning. On Sundays, many museums offer free entry; the Hungarian National Museum is a solid choice.

Thermal Baths on a Budget

Budapest’s thermal baths are a must. Széchenyi Baths costs $18 for a full day—arrive right at 9 a.m. to avoid crowds. For a cheaper option, try Rudas Baths ($14) on weekday mornings. Bring your own towel and flip-flops to avoid rental fees ($5 extra).

Nightlife Without the Price Tag

Ruin bars like Szimpla Kert are iconic. Drinks cost $3–$5, and entry is free. For a local vibe, head to Instant-Frida Complex, a sprawling ruin bar with multiple rooms. Stick to beer and wine—cocktails can push $8–$10. End the night with a $2 slice of pizza from a street vendor.

Money-Saving Tips Summary

Use the Budapest Card ($25 for 72 hours) for free public transport and museum discounts, but only if you plan to visit three or more paid attractions. Otherwise, buy a 72-hour transport pass for $15. Always pay in forints (HUF) to avoid dynamic currency conversion fees. With these tricks, you’ll see Budapest without blowing your budget.
